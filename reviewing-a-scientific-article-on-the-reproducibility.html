6 Reviewing a scientific article on the reproducibility

I will be reviewing the article The d Factor: General Distractibility and Its Relationships with ADHD Symptoms and Hyperfocus1. To review how reproducible the article is i will be basing my review on criteria set by article Reproducibility and reporting practices in COVID-19 preprint manuscripts2. The criteria set by the article can be found in table 1.

6.0.1 Criteria

Table 1: Criteria for the scoring of the reproducibility of a article.

Transparency Criteria Definition Response Type
Study Purpose A concise statement in the introduction of the article, often in the last paragraph, that establishes the reason the research was conducted. Also called the study objective. Binary (Yes or No)
Data Availability Statement A statement, in an individual section offset from the main body of text, that explains how or if one can access a study’s data. The title of the section may vary, but it must explicitly mention data; it is therefore distinct from a supplementary materials section. Binary (Yes or No)
Data Location Where the article’s data can be accessed, either raw or processed. Found Value
Study Location Author has stated in the methods section where the study took place or the data’s country/region of origin. Binary (Yes or No); Found Value
Author Review The professionalism of the contact information that the author has provided in the manuscript. Found Value
Ethics Statement A statement within the manuscript indicating any ethical concerns, including the presence of sensitive data. Binary (Yes or No)
Funding Statement A statement within the manuscript indicating whether or not the authors received funding for their research. Binary (Yes or No)
Code Availability Authors have shared access to the most updated code that they used in their study, including code used for analysis. Binary (Yes or No)

6.1 The article review

The d Factor: General Distractibility and Its Relationships with ADHD Symptoms and Hyperfocus1

Research question: How can we best characterize individual differences in distractibility?

The article examines the vulnerability to distractions between different individuals. Primarily focusing on understanding the relationships between popular constructs that tap into distractibility. These constructs included external distraction, thought intrusions, repetitive negative thinking, and mind-wandering. Also examining the plausibility of extracting a general factor to capture the commonality underlying these constructs. The second goal of the study was to apply the model of distractibility derived from the study to understand the nature of ADHD symptomatology and hyperfocus. The article examined the role of construct-specific variances in explaining ADHD symptoms, functional impairments, and hyperfocus. Besides that the article examined how much additional variance the specific distractibility factors could explain over and above the general factor.

Methods:

The study involved two independent sample groups. Sample 1 included 651 adults and sample 2 included 569 students. Each individual participant completed an online battery of self-report questionnaires measuring distractibility, intrusive thoughts, repetitive negative thinking, mind-wandering, ADHD symptoms, and hyperfocus.

Multiple validated scales were used (e.g., IPI-D, ASQ-E, WBSI, PTQ, PSWQ, Adult ADHD Self-report Scale), each scored by averaging item responses, with higher scores reflecting greater symptom severity. To ensure consistency in interpretation some scales were partially used or adjusted (e.g., reverse-scoring).

Data analysis was performed in R using confirmatory factor analysis and structural equation modeling (SEM) via the lavaan package. Model fit was assessed using standard indices (CFI, RMSEA, SRMR), and model comparisons were evaluated using Satorra-Bentler χ² tests and Bayes Factors based on BIC. Sample 1 was used for model exploration, and Sample 2 was used for replication.

6.3 Reproduction review of R code

To recreate Figure 1 i did not need to change any of the given code to create this heatmap. I did however need to make sure that the function func.R was loaded and in the correct directory. I also had to change the path to load the data and image to make it compatible with this Rmarkdown page.

Analyzing the code of the data analysis I feel that a lot of steps were being reused. These reused steps could probably become a function for easier analyzing of the data. Because of all these steps it seemed like a unnecessarily long analysis. A lot of the outcome of the split up steps would probably have been clearer when presented together instead of split up.

I did thoroughly enjoy the way the author presented the different goals within the data analysis. It very clearly showed the next subject of the analysis.

I would give the reproducibility of the code a 4.5
